Cryo-ET Blog

Workflow, Classical Reconstruction Methods, and Advanced Deep Learning Approaches

Cryogenic electron tomography, or Cryo-ET, is a powerful imaging technique for observing biological structures in their near-native cellular environments. However, reconstructing high-quality 3D tomograms from limited-angle, noisy projection data remains a challenging inverse problem. This blog introduces the basic Cryo-ET workflow, reviews classical reconstruction methods such as Weighted Back Projection (WBP) and SIRT, and discusses recent advanced reconstruction methods including IsoNet, DeepDeWedge, CryoGEN, and CryoGEN-II.

1. Cryo-ET Background

1.1 The Pixel Bottleneck of Traditional Biology

For a long time, optical microscopy was the standard tool for observing biological samples. However, because visible light has wavelengths of roughly hundreds of nanometers, optical microscopes are limited by the diffraction limit. In practice, this means that structures smaller than about 200 nm are difficult to resolve clearly. Many important biological objects, such as viruses, protein complexes, and fine intracellular structures, exist at the nanometer scale, beyond the effective resolution of traditional optical microscopy.

1.2 The Birth of Electron Microscopy

To overcome the resolution limit of light microscopy, researchers began to use electrons instead of visible light for imaging. In 1931, Ernst Ruska built the first transmission electron microscope, or TEM. Because accelerated electrons have much shorter wavelengths than visible light, electron microscopy greatly improved the resolving power of microscopes and opened the door to observing biological structures at much smaller scales.

This was a major leap for biology. The observable scale moved from the cellular level toward the nanoscale, making it possible to visualize objects such as viruses, bacteriophages, and detailed intracellular structures that were previously inaccessible to optical microscopy.

1.3 TEM Imaging: Projection Rather Than Reflection

Unlike optical imaging that often relies on reflected or emitted light, TEM forms images through projection. An electron gun emits electrons, which are accelerated by a high voltage and transmitted through a thin specimen. When electrons pass through the sample, they interact with atoms inside the specimen. Denser regions scatter more electrons, so fewer electrons reach the detector and these regions appear darker. Less dense regions allow more electrons to pass through and appear brighter.

However, a single TEM image is still only a two-dimensional projection of a three-dimensional object. Depth information is collapsed along the direction of the electron beam. As a result, although TEM can provide very high-resolution 2D images, it cannot directly recover the full 3D structure from a single view.

1.4 From Electron Microscopy to Cryogenic Electron Microscopy

Although electron microscopy greatly improves resolution, traditional electron microscopy is not ideal for biological samples. Biological specimens are fragile: under high-energy electron beams, they can be damaged quickly; in the vacuum environment of the microscope, water can evaporate, causing the sample to collapse or deform. These problems make it difficult to observe biological structures in their native state.

The key breakthrough of cryogenic electron microscopy was rapid freezing. In the 1980s, Jacques Dubochet developed vitrification-based sample preparation. By rapidly cooling the specimen, water is transformed into vitreous, or glass-like, ice instead of crystalline ice. This preserves biological molecules in a near-native hydrated state and avoids damage caused by ice crystal formation.

Together with later advances such as direct electron detectors and powerful computational algorithms, cryo-EM made it possible to reconstruct high-resolution biological structures from many noisy, low-dose images.

1.5 From 2D Images to 3D Structures

The next key question is how to recover three-dimensional structures from two-dimensional electron microscopy images. In cryo-EM single-particle analysis, or Cryo-EM SPA, researchers image a large number of purified copies of the same molecule. These 2D particle images are aligned, classified, and averaged to reconstruct a high-resolution 3D structure of the molecule.

Cryogenic electron tomography, or Cryo-ET, follows a different strategy. Instead of relying on many purified copies of a molecule, Cryo-ET rotates the sample and collects a series of projection images from different tilt angles. These projections are then computationally reconstructed into a 3D tomogram. This makes Cryo-ET especially suitable for studying cells, tissues, and in situ biological samples.

Cryo-EM Single Particle Analysis (Cryo-EM SPA) Cryogenic Electron Tomography (Cryo-ET)
Observed object Purified protein solution, often containing hundreds of thousands to millions of copies of the same molecule. Cells, tissues, or in situ biological samples with complex native context.
Data logic Collect many 2D particle images, then align, classify, and average them to reconstruct a 3D model of a single molecule. Rotate the sample, collect projection images from multiple tilt angles, and reconstruct a 3D model of the whole specimen.
Scale Microscopic, usually at the molecular level. Intermediate, reaching the nanoscale while preserving the position of molecules inside cells.

1.6 Why Cryo-ET Matters

The major advantage of Cryo-ET is that it allows biological structures to be observed in their native cellular context. This is important for in situ structural biology, where the goal is not only to determine the structure of an isolated molecule, but also to understand where it is located, how it interacts with surrounding structures, and how it functions inside the cell.

Compared with Cryo-EM SPA, Cryo-ET is better suited for capturing rare events, low-abundance complexes, and heterogeneous cellular architectures. It provides a bridge between molecular-level mechanisms and cellular-scale organization, making it a powerful tool for studying biological systems across scales.

工作流程、经典重建方法与先进深度学习方法

冷冻电子断层扫描（Cryo-ET）是一种能够在接近原位状态下观察生物结构的重要成像技术。然而，由于倾斜角度受限、电子剂量低以及噪声较强，从二维投影重建高质量三维 tomogram 仍然是一个具有挑战性的逆问题。本文将从 Cryo-ET 的基本工作流程出发，介绍 Weighted Back Projection（WBP）、SIRT 等经典重建方法，并进一步讨论 IsoNet、DeepDeWedge、CryoGEN 和 CryoGEN-II 等 advanced reconstruction methods。

1. Cryo-ET 背景知识

1.1 传统生物学的像素瓶颈

长期以来，光学显微镜是观察生物样本的主要工具。然而，可见光的波长通常在数百纳米量级，因此光学显微镜受到衍射极限的限制。在实际成像中，大约 200 nm 以下的结构很难被清楚分辨。而许多重要的生物对象，例如病毒、蛋白质复合物以及细胞内部的精细结构，都处于纳米尺度，超出了传统光学显微镜的有效观测范围。

1.2 电子显微镜的诞生

为了突破光学显微镜的分辨率限制，研究者开始使用电子束代替可见光进行成像。1931 年，Ernst Ruska 制造了第一台透射电子显微镜（Transmission Electron Microscope, TEM）。由于加速电子的波长远短于可见光，电子显微镜显著提升了显微成像的分辨能力，使人类能够观察到更微观的生物结构。

这对生物学来说是一次重要飞跃。显微观测的尺度从细胞层面进一步推进到纳米尺度，使研究者能够观察病毒、噬菌体以及细胞内部精细结构等传统光学显微镜难以触及的对象。

1.3 TEM 成像：投射而非反射

与许多依赖反射光或发射光的光学成像不同，TEM 的成像方式是投射成像。电子枪发射电子后，电子在高电压下被加速，并穿过一个很薄的样本。当电子穿过样本时，会与样本中的原子发生相互作用。电子密度较高的区域会散射更多电子，因此到达探测器的电子更少，在图像中表现为较暗区域；电子密度较低的区域则允许更多电子穿过，在图像中表现为较亮区域。

然而，单张 TEM 图像本质上仍然是三维物体在电子束方向上的二维投影。样本的深度信息会沿着电子束方向被压缩。因此，虽然 TEM 能够提供高分辨率的二维图像，但单个视角无法直接恢复完整的三维结构。

1.4 从电子显微镜到冷冻电镜

尽管电子显微镜极大提升了分辨率，传统电镜并不天然适合观察生物样本。生物样本非常脆弱：在高能电子束下容易受到辐照损伤；在电镜的真空环境中，水分会蒸发，导致样本塌陷或形态失真。因此，传统电镜很难保持生物结构的天然状态。

冷冻电镜的关键突破是快速冷冻。20 世纪 80 年代，Jacques Dubochet 发展了基于玻璃化的样本制备方法。通过快速降温，样本中的水不会形成晶态冰，而是转变为玻璃态冰。这种方式可以将生物大分子保存在接近天然的含水状态，同时避免冰晶形成对结构造成破坏。

随着直接电子探测器和计算算法的发展，冷冻电镜进一步实现了从大量低剂量、低信噪比图像中重建高分辨率生物结构的能力。

1.5 从二维图像到三维结构

接下来的关键问题是：如何从二维电镜图像恢复三维结构？在冷冻电镜单颗粒分析（Cryo-EM Single Particle Analysis, Cryo-EM SPA）中，研究者会对大量纯化后的同一种分子颗粒进行成像。随后，通过对这些二维颗粒图像进行对齐、分类和平均，可以重建出该分子的高分辨率三维结构。

冷冻电子断层扫描（Cryogenic Electron Tomography, Cryo-ET）采用的是另一种策略。它并不依赖大量纯化后的分子拷贝，而是通过旋转样本，从不同倾斜角度采集一系列投影图像。随后，这些投影图像会被计算重建为三维 tomogram。因此，Cryo-ET 特别适合研究细胞、组织以及原位生物样本。

冷冻电镜单颗粒分析（Cryo-EM SPA） 冷冻电子断层扫描（Cryo-ET）

1.6 为什么 Cryo-ET 重要

Cryo-ET 的主要优势在于，它能够在接近真实的细胞环境中观察生物结构。这对于原位结构生物学非常重要，因为研究目标不仅是解析某个孤立分子的结构，还包括理解它在细胞中的位置、与周围结构的相互作用，以及它在真实细胞环境中的功能。

与 Cryo-EM SPA 相比，Cryo-ET 更适合捕捉稀有事件、低丰度复合物以及异质性的细胞结构。它能够连接分子尺度机制与细胞尺度组织结构，是研究跨尺度生物系统的重要工具。
