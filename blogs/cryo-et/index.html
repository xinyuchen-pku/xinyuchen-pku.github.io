Cryo-ET Blog

Workflow, Classical Reconstruction Methods, and Advanced Deep Learning Approaches

Cryogenic electron tomography, or Cryo-ET, is a powerful imaging technique for observing biological structures in their near-native cellular environments. However, reconstructing high-quality 3D tomograms from limited-angle, noisy projection data remains a challenging inverse problem. This blog introduces the basic Cryo-ET workflow, reviews classical reconstruction methods such as Weighted Back Projection (WBP) and SIRT, and discusses recent advanced reconstruction methods including IsoNet, DeepDeWedge, CryoGEN, and CryoGEN-II.

1. Cryo-ET Background

1.1 The Pixel Bottleneck of Traditional Biology

For a long time, optical microscopy was the standard tool for observing biological samples. However, because visible light has wavelengths of roughly hundreds of nanometers, optical microscopes are limited by the diffraction limit. In practice, this means that structures smaller than about 200 nm are difficult to resolve clearly. Many important biological objects, such as viruses, protein complexes, and fine intracellular structures, exist at the nanometer scale, beyond the effective resolution of traditional optical microscopy.

1.2 The Birth of Electron Microscopy

To overcome the resolution limit of light microscopy, researchers began to use electrons instead of visible light for imaging. In 1931, Ernst Ruska built the first transmission electron microscope, or TEM. Because accelerated electrons have much shorter wavelengths than visible light, electron microscopy greatly improved the resolving power of microscopes and opened the door to observing biological structures at much smaller scales.

This was a major leap for biology. The observable scale moved from the cellular level toward the nanoscale, making it possible to visualize objects such as viruses, bacteriophages, and detailed intracellular structures that were previously inaccessible to optical microscopy.

1.3 TEM Imaging: Projection Rather Than Reflection

Unlike optical imaging that often relies on reflected or emitted light, TEM forms images through projection. An electron gun emits electrons, which are accelerated by a high voltage and transmitted through a thin specimen. When electrons pass through the sample, they interact with atoms inside the specimen. Denser regions scatter more electrons, so fewer electrons reach the detector and these regions appear darker. Less dense regions allow more electrons to pass through and appear brighter.

However, a single TEM image is still only a two-dimensional projection of a three-dimensional object. Depth information is collapsed along the direction of the electron beam. As a result, although TEM can provide very high-resolution 2D images, it cannot directly recover the full 3D structure from a single view.

1.4 From Electron Microscopy to Cryogenic Electron Microscopy

Although electron microscopy greatly improves resolution, traditional electron microscopy is not ideal for biological samples. Biological specimens are fragile: under high-energy electron beams, they can be damaged quickly; in the vacuum environment of the microscope, water can evaporate, causing the sample to collapse or deform. These problems make it difficult to observe biological structures in their native state.

The key breakthrough of cryogenic electron microscopy was rapid freezing. In the 1980s, Jacques Dubochet developed vitrification-based sample preparation. By rapidly cooling the specimen, water is transformed into vitreous, or glass-like, ice instead of crystalline ice. This preserves biological molecules in a near-native hydrated state and avoids damage caused by ice crystal formation.

Together with later advances such as direct electron detectors and powerful computational algorithms, cryo-EM made it possible to reconstruct high-resolution biological structures from many noisy, low-dose images.

1.5 From 2D Images to 3D Structures

The next key question is how to recover three-dimensional structures from two-dimensional electron microscopy images. In cryo-EM single-particle analysis, or Cryo-EM SPA, researchers image a large number of purified copies of the same molecule. These 2D particle images are aligned, classified, and averaged to reconstruct a high-resolution 3D structure of the molecule.

Cryogenic electron tomography, or Cryo-ET, follows a different strategy. Instead of relying on many purified copies of a molecule, Cryo-ET rotates the sample and collects a series of projection images from different tilt angles. These projections are then computationally reconstructed into a 3D tomogram. This makes Cryo-ET especially suitable for studying cells, tissues, and in situ biological samples.

1.6 Why Cryo-ET Matters

The major advantage of Cryo-ET is that it allows biological structures to be observed in their native cellular context. This is important for in situ structural biology, where the goal is not only to determine the structure of an isolated molecule, but also to understand where it is located, how it interacts with surrounding structures, and how it functions inside the cell.

Compared with Cryo-EM SPA, Cryo-ET is better suited for capturing rare events, low-abundance complexes, and heterogeneous cellular architectures. It provides a bridge between molecular-level mechanisms and cellular-scale organization, making it a powerful tool for studying biological systems across scales.

2. Cryo-ET Workflow

A typical Cryo-ET workflow consists of five major steps: sampling, data collection, tilt series assembly, optional tilt series correction, and tomogram reconstruction.

2.1 Sampling

Sampling is the first step in Cryo-ET. Its goal is to prepare a thin, well-preserved biological specimen that can be imaged under cryogenic conditions in a transmission electron microscope. Since biological samples are usually thick, hydrated, and sensitive to radiation damage, careful sample preparation is essential for preserving native structures and enabling high-quality downstream reconstruction.

The process usually begins with vitrification. The specimen is rapidly frozen by high-pressure freezing or plunge freezing, so that water is transformed into vitreous ice instead of crystalline ice. This avoids ice crystal damage and helps preserve biological molecules in a near-native state.

After vitrification, the sample is transferred under cryogenic conditions for localization and thinning. Cryo-CLEM can be used to locate the region of interest, and FIB-SEM is then used to mill away excess material. Before milling, a protective layer such as platinum or an organometallic coating is often deposited to reduce surface damage.

Finally, low-current polishing is performed to reduce curtaining artifacts and obtain a smooth, thin lamella. The final product is a thin sample slice suitable for subsequent tilt-series data collection in the TEM.

2.2 Data Collection

After the thin lamella is prepared, the cryo-sample grid is inserted into a transmission electron microscope equipped with a tilting stage. During data collection, the sample is gradually tilted around a fixed tilt axis, and a projection image is recorded at each tilt angle. Instead of directly observing the full 3D structure, Cryo-ET collects a series of 2D projections from different viewing directions.

In practice, the tilt range is limited, often around −60° to +60°. This limitation is caused by the geometry of the microscope, the sample holder, and the increasing effective thickness of the sample at high tilt angles. Because projections outside this angular range cannot be collected, part of the information in Fourier space is missing. This later becomes the well-known missing wedge problem in tomogram reconstruction.

At each tilt angle, the detector usually records a short movie stack rather than a single still image. For example, one projection may consist of 3 to 7 frames. These frames can later be aligned and averaged to reduce beam-induced motion and improve the signal-to-noise ratio.

  • Unidirectional scheme. Suppose the tilt range is from −60° to +60° with a step size of 3°. A unidirectional scheme may collect images continuously from one end to the other, such as −60°, −57°, −54°, ..., 0°, ..., +57°, +60°. This is simple and fast, but the important low-tilt images near 0° may be collected after the sample has already received a considerable electron dose.
  • Bidirectional scheme. A bidirectional scheme starts near 0° and then collects one side before returning to the other side, for example 0°, +3°, +6°, ..., +60°, followed by −3°, −6°, ..., −60°. This protects the low-tilt images by collecting them early, but the two sides of the tilt series may have different accumulated dose levels, leading to asymmetric data quality.
  • Dose-symmetric scheme. A dose-symmetric scheme alternates between positive and negative angles around 0°, such as 0°, +3°, −3°, +6°, −6°, ..., +60°, −60°. This strategy keeps the accumulated dose more balanced across opposite tilt angles and usually gives more symmetric data quality. However, it requires frequent mechanical switching of the stage and therefore places higher demands on microscope stability and acquisition control.

Overall, data collection determines both the angular coverage and the noise level of the final tilt series. These two factors directly affect the quality of the reconstructed tomogram: limited angular coverage leads to missing wedge artifacts, while low-dose imaging introduces strong noise. Therefore, data collection is not only an experimental step, but also a key factor that shapes the computational reconstruction problem.

2.3 Tilt Series Assembly

After data collection, each tilt angle usually corresponds to a short movie stack rather than a single image. Before these data can be used for tomographic reconstruction, beam-induced motion and radiation damage must be corrected. In practice, this step is commonly performed using MotionCor2 and MotionCor3, two widely used motion-correction tools in the cryo-EM and Cryo-ET community. More information about these tools can be found at https://emcore.ucsf.edu/ucsf-software.

These methods transform raw movie stacks into motion-corrected projection images through several key operations:

  • Global motion correction. During imaging, the entire field of view may drift because of beam-induced motion, mechanical instability, charging effects, or deformation of the vitreous ice layer. The first step estimates the overall displacement of each frame and shifts all frames into a common coordinate system.
  • Local or patch-based correction. Motion is often not uniform across the image. Different regions may move in different directions or by different amounts. To address this, the image is divided into multiple patches, and local motion trajectories are estimated separately. These local measurements are then combined into a smooth motion field across the entire image, allowing local deformation to be corrected without introducing artificial discontinuities between neighboring patches.
  • Frame summation. After motion correction, the aligned frames within a movie stack are combined into a single projection image. Averaging multiple frames suppresses random noise while preserving structural information that remains consistent throughout the exposure.
  • Dose weighting. As the accumulated electron dose increases, later frames become progressively more affected by radiation damage, especially at high spatial frequencies. Dose weighting therefore assigns different frequency-dependent weights to different frames, preserving reliable high-resolution information while reducing radiation-damaged signal.

After these operations, each tilt angle is represented by a single corrected projection image. The corrected projections are then assembled according to their acquisition angles to form the final tilt series, which serves as the input for downstream tilt-series alignment and tomographic reconstruction.

2.4 Tilt Series Correction

Tilt series correction is an optional but important preprocessing step in Cryo-ET. Its main purpose is to correct the distortions introduced by the electron microscope imaging system before tomographic reconstruction. One of the most important corrections is CTF correction, where CTF stands for contrast transfer function.

Source of CTF

In cryo-EM and Cryo-ET imaging, the microscope is often intentionally operated with a small defocus. Defocus enhances phase contrast, making weakly scattering biological samples more visible. However, this also means that the scattered and unscattered electron waves accumulate different phase shifts before reaching the detector.

The effect of the electron optical system on different spatial frequencies is described by the contrast transfer function. A commonly used form of the CTF is

\[ \mathrm{CTF}(k)= \left[ -\sqrt{1-A^2}\sin \chi(k) +A\cos \chi(k) \right] \cdot e^{-Bk^2/4}. \]

Here, \(k\) denotes spatial frequency, \(A\) is the amplitude contrast ratio, and \(e^{-Bk^2/4}\) is an envelope term that describes the decay of high-frequency information. The phase shift \(\chi(k)\) is usually determined by parameters such as defocus, electron wavelength, and spherical aberration:

\[ \chi(k)= \pi \lambda \Delta f k^2 +\frac{\pi}{2}C_s\lambda^3 k^4. \]

Here, \(\lambda\) is the electron wavelength, \(\Delta f\) is the defocus value, and \(C_s\) is the spherical aberration coefficient.

The CTF mainly causes two problems. First, it can become negative at certain spatial frequencies, which leads to phase flipping: bright and dark density contrasts are reversed. Second, the envelope term suppresses high-frequency signals, making fine structural details weaker or blurred.

Wiener Filter for CTF Correction

To understand CTF correction, we can write the imaging process in Fourier space as

\[ Y(k)=H(k)X(k)+N(k), \]

where \(Y(k)\) is the observed image in Fourier space, \(X(k)\) is the unknown clean signal, \(H(k)\) is the CTF, and \(N(k)\) is noise.

A direct inverse would estimate the clean signal as

\[ \hat{X}(k)=\frac{Y(k)}{H(k)}. \]

However, this is unstable. When \(H(k)\) is close to zero, even a small amount of noise will be greatly amplified. Therefore, instead of directly dividing by the CTF, we use a Wiener filter, which balances signal recovery and noise suppression.

Assume that we estimate \(X(k)\) using a linear filter \(G(k)\):

\[ \hat{X}(k)=G(k)Y(k). \]

The goal is to choose \(G(k)\) to minimize the mean squared error:

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[|\hat{X}(k)-X(k)|^2\right]. \]

Substituting \(Y(k)=H(k)X(k)+N(k)\), we get

\[ \hat{X}(k)-X(k)= (G(k)H(k)-1)X(k)+G(k)N(k). \]

Assuming that the clean signal and noise are uncorrelated, the expected error becomes

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[|\hat{X}(k)-X(k)|^2\right] = |G(k)H(k)-1|^2 S_X(k) + |G(k)|^2 S_N(k), \]

where \(S_X(k)\) and \(S_N(k)\) are the power spectra of the clean signal and noise.

Minimizing this expression with respect to \(G(k)\) gives the Wiener filter:

\[ G(k)= \frac{H^*(k)S_X(k)} {|H(k)|^2S_X(k)+S_N(k)}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ G(k)= \frac{H^*(k)} {|H(k)|^2+\frac{S_N(k)}{S_X(k)}}. \]

Therefore, the Wiener-filtered estimate is

\[ \hat{X}(k)= \frac{H^*(k)} {|H(k)|^2+\frac{S_N(k)}{S_X(k)}} Y(k). \]

When \(H(k)\) is large and the signal-to-noise ratio is high, the Wiener filter behaves similarly to inverse filtering. When \(H(k)\) is small or the noise level is high, the denominator prevents excessive noise amplification. This is why Wiener filtering is more stable than direct CTF inversion.

2.5 Tomogram Reconstruction

After sampling, data collection, tilt series assembly, and optional correction, the final goal of Cryo-ET is to reconstruct a 3D tomogram from a series of 2D projection images. This is the core computational step of the workflow: given projections from different tilt angles, we want to recover the underlying 3D structure of the specimen.

Weighted Back Projection

The mathematical foundation of tomographic reconstruction is the central slice theorem. In the 2D case, suppose the object is \(f(x,y)\), and its projection at angle \(\theta\) is \(p_\theta(t)\). The projection can be written as integrating the object along lines perpendicular to the projection direction:

\[ p_\theta(t)= \int\int f(x,y)\, \delta(t-x\cos\theta-y\sin\theta)\,dx\,dy. \]

Taking the one-dimensional Fourier transform of this projection gives

\[ P_\theta(k)= \int p_\theta(t)e^{-i2\pi kt}\,dt. \]

According to the central slice theorem, this is exactly equal to a slice through the two-dimensional Fourier transform of the object:

\[ P_\theta(k)= F(k\cos\theta,k\sin\theta). \]

In other words, each projection provides one radial line in Fourier space. If we collect enough projections from different angles, these Fourier slices can be combined to recover the original object.

A simple idea is back projection. For each projection \(p_\theta(t)\), we smear its value back along the corresponding projection direction and sum the contributions from all angles:

\[ f_{\mathrm{BP}}(x,y)= \int_0^\pi p_\theta(x\cos\theta+y\sin\theta) \,d\theta. \]

However, simple back projection produces a blurry reconstruction. The reason is that Fourier-space samples are not uniformly distributed. All radial slices pass through the origin, so low-frequency components near the origin are sampled many times, while high-frequency components farther away are sampled much more sparsely. This overemphasizes low-frequency information and makes the reconstructed image blurred.

Weighted Back Projection, or WBP, corrects this imbalance by applying a frequency-domain filter before back projection. The most common filter is the ramp filter, which multiplies each projection spectrum by \(|k|\). This suppresses the overrepresented low-frequency components and enhances high-frequency details:

\[ \tilde{P}_\theta(k)= |k|P_\theta(k). \]

After filtering, the reconstructed image can be written as

\[ f(x,y)= \int_0^\pi \left[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P_\theta(k)|k| e^{i2\pi k(x\cos\theta+y\sin\theta)} \,dk \right] d\theta. \]

This is the basic idea of WBP: first filter each projection in Fourier space, then back-project the filtered projections into real space and accumulate them over all angles.

Simultaneous Iterative Reconstruction Technique

Another classical reconstruction method is Simultaneous Iterative Reconstruction Technique, or SIRT. Unlike WBP, which is based on direct analytical inversion, SIRT formulates tomographic reconstruction as a linear inverse problem.

After discretization, the unknown tomogram can be represented as a vector \(x\), and the measured projection data can be represented as a vector \(b\). The imaging process is modeled as

\[ Ax=b, \]

where \(A\) is the system projection matrix. Each row of \(A\) describes how one projection ray passes through the voxels of the volume, and each element \(a_{ij}\) describes the contribution of voxel \(j\) to ray \(i\).

In practice, \(A\) is large, sparse, and ill-conditioned, and the number of equations is often not exactly equal to the number of unknowns. Therefore, directly solving \(A^{-1}b\) is not feasible. Instead, reconstruction is usually formulated as a least-squares problem:

\[ \min_x f(x)= \frac{1}{2}\|Ax-b\|_2^2. \]

The gradient of this objective is

\[ \nabla f(x)= A^T(Ax-b). \]

A basic gradient descent update would therefore be

\[ x^{(k+1)} = x^{(k)} + \lambda A^T(b-Ax^{(k)}), \]

where \(b-Ax^{(k)}\) is the projection residual, and \(\lambda\) is the step size.

SIRT further introduces row and column normalization to balance the contribution of different rays and different voxels. Let \(R\) be a diagonal matrix containing row sums of \(A\), and \(C\) be a diagonal matrix containing column sums of \(A\):

\[ R_{ii}=\sum_j a_{ij}, \qquad C_{jj}=\sum_i a_{ij}. \]

With these normalization terms, the SIRT update becomes

\[ x^{(k+1)} = x^{(k)} + \lambda C^{-1}A^TR^{-1} \left(b-Ax^{(k)}\right). \]

This update has an intuitive interpretation. First, the current estimate \(x^{(k)}\) is forward-projected to obtain \(Ax^{(k)}\). Then the difference between the measured projection and the predicted projection is computed. Finally, this residual is back-projected into the volume to update the tomogram. Repeating this process gradually reduces the projection error.

Compared with WBP, SIRT often produces smoother reconstructions and can suppress some streak artifacts. However, it is computationally more expensive because it requires many forward-projection and back-projection iterations.

Why Classical Reconstruction Is Not Enough

Although WBP and SIRT are fundamental methods in tomographic reconstruction, they cannot fully solve the challenges of Cryo-ET. The first major issue comes from data collection. Because the specimen can usually only be tilted within a limited angular range, such as approximately −60° to +60°, projection data from many directions are missing.

In Fourier space, this limited-angle acquisition creates a missing wedge: a wedge-shaped region of unsampled frequency information. Since this information was never measured, classical reconstruction algorithms cannot truly recover it from the data alone. As a result, reconstructed tomograms often contain anisotropic resolution, elongation artifacts, and missing structural details.

The second issue is noise. Cryo-ET must use low electron doses to avoid damaging biological samples, but low-dose imaging leads to low signal-to-noise ratio. During reconstruction, noise can be amplified or spread across the volume, making the final tomogram noisy and low-contrast.

WBP is fast and simple, but it is sensitive to missing angles and noise. SIRT is more robust and often gives smoother results, but it still cannot reconstruct information that is absent from the measured tilt series. Therefore, both methods are limited by the physical constraints of Cryo-ET data acquisition.

This is why more advanced reconstruction methods are needed. To better recover structural details, reduce artifacts, and support downstream biological analysis, recent methods have started to incorporate deep learning, self-supervision, denoising, and generative priors into Cryo-ET reconstruction.

3. Advanced Reconstruction Methods

3.1 IsoNet

IsoNet is a self-supervised deep learning method designed to reduce missing-wedge artifacts and improve the isotropy of Cryo-ET tomograms. Instead of requiring clean ground truth or subtomogram averaging, IsoNet learns structural information directly from the raw tomograms themselves.

The workflow of IsoNet contains five steps: optional CTF deconvolution, optional mask generation, subtomogram extraction, iterative training, and prediction. The optional CTF deconvolution step enhances contrast, while mask generation excludes empty regions such as ice, carbon, or vacuum. Then, subtomograms are extracted from informative regions and used for self-supervised training.

The key idea of IsoNet is to create training pairs from the tomogram itself. For an extracted subtomogram \(x\), IsoNet first rotates it into a new orientation:

\[ x_r = R_r x, \]

where \(R_r\) denotes a 3D rotation operator.

Then, an additional artificial missing wedge mask \(M_a\) is applied in Fourier space to generate a degraded input:

\[ \tilde{x}_r = \mathcal{F}^{-1} \left[ M_a \cdot \mathcal{F}(x_r) \right], \]

where \(\mathcal{F}\) and \(\mathcal{F}^{-1}\) denote Fourier transform and inverse Fourier transform. The degraded subtomogram \(\tilde{x}_r\) is used as the input, while the rotated subtomogram \(x_r\) is used as the target.

The neural network \(f_\theta\) is trained to recover the artificially removed information by minimizing a reconstruction loss:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{IsoNet}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x,r} \left[ \left\| f_\theta(\tilde{x}_r)-x_r \right\|_2^2 \right]. \]

In this way, IsoNet learns a mapping from a more incomplete subtomogram to a less incomplete one, without requiring an external clean ground truth.

This training process is performed iteratively. After each iteration, the trained network predicts corrected subtomograms. Importantly, IsoNet does not overwrite the originally observed Fourier information. Instead, it only adds the newly predicted information into the original missing-wedge region. If \(M_o\) denotes the original observed Fourier region, the update can be written conceptually as

\[ \mathcal{F}(x^{(t+1)}) = M_o \cdot \mathcal{F}(x^{(0)}) + (1-M_o)\cdot \mathcal{F}(\hat{x}^{(t)}), \]

where \(x^{(0)}\) is the original subtomogram and \(\hat{x}^{(t)}\) is the network prediction at iteration \(t\).

Through this recursive refinement, missing-wedge artifacts are gradually reduced and the reconstruction becomes more isotropic. In practice, IsoNet can reduce artifacts, improve contrast, and make important structures easier to interpret. For example, structures that appear elongated, smeared, or incomplete in the original tomogram may become more complete and clearer after IsoNet processing.

3.2 DeepDeWedge

DeepDeWedge is another self-supervised deep learning method for Cryo-ET reconstruction. Compared with IsoNet, its main focus is not only missing-wedge compensation, but also simultaneous denoising. In other words, DeepDeWedge aims to recover missing structural information while producing a cleaner and higher-contrast tomogram.

The key idea comes from the Noise2Noise principle: if two observations contain the same underlying signal but independent noise, a neural network can be trained to map one noisy observation to the other without requiring a clean ground truth. In Cryo-ET, DeepDeWedge obtains such paired observations by splitting the original tilt series into two sub-tilt-series, such as even and odd tilts.

The workflow can be summarized as follows:

  • The original tilt series is split into two sub-tilt-series, denoted as \(t^0\) and \(t^1\). Each sub-tilt-series is reconstructed separately with filtered back projection, producing two noisy tomograms that share the same biological structure but contain different noise patterns.
  • Paired subtomograms \((v_i^0, v_i^1)\) are extracted from the two reconstructions. These pairs provide the self-supervised training signal: one half-reconstruction can be used as the input, while the other half-reconstruction serves as the target.
  • During model fitting, DeepDeWedge applies rotations and Fourier masks to the paired subtomograms. This allows the network to learn both how to suppress noise and how to fill in missing-wedge regions. A simplified training objective can be written as
\[ \min_\theta \sum_i \left\| f_\theta(\tilde{v}_i^0)-\tilde{v}_i^1 \right\|_2^2, \]

where \(\tilde{v}_i^0\) and \(\tilde{v}_i^1\) denote the transformed input-target pair, and \(f_\theta\) is the neural network.

  • After training converges, the fitted model is applied back to the subtomograms and the refined subtomograms are reassembled into tomograms. The two refined half-reconstructions are then averaged to obtain the final reconstruction.

The advantage of DeepDeWedge is that it combines two previously separated goals: denoising and missing-wedge reconstruction. Compared with using a denoiser and a missing-wedge correction method as two separate steps, DeepDeWedge provides a unified self-supervised framework and often produces cleaner tomograms with stronger overall contrast.

3.3 CryoGEN

CryoGEN rethinks Cryo-ET reconstruction from a probabilistic and generative perspective. Instead of treating missing-wedge correction as only a deterministic image-to-image restoration problem, CryoGEN formulates Cryo-ET reconstruction as an ill-posed inverse problem with multiple plausible solutions.

Let \(y\) denote the observed missing-wedge tomogram, and let \(x\) denote the underlying complete tomogram that we would like to recover. The forward degradation process can be written as

\[ y=T_M(x)+\epsilon_n, \qquad \epsilon_n\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_n^2I), \]

where \(T_M\) is the missing-wedge operator and \(\epsilon_n\) is imaging noise. Intuitively, \(T_M\) removes part of the Fourier-space information according to the limited tilt range.

From Bayes' rule, the posterior distribution of the complete tomogram can be written as

\[ \log p(x|y)=\log p(y|x)+\log p(x)+C. \]

Since the noise is assumed to be Gaussian, the likelihood term becomes a data-consistency term:

\[ \log p(y|x)= -\frac{1}{2\sigma_n^2} \left\|T_M(x)-y\right\|_2^2 +C. \]

Therefore, the maximum a posteriori, or MAP, reconstruction can be written as

\[ x^*(y)= \arg\max_x \left[ -\frac{1}{2\sigma_n^2} \left\|T_M(x)-y\right\|_2^2 +\log p(x) \right]. \]

This equation contains two parts. The first term requires the reconstructed tomogram to be consistent with the observed missing-wedge tomogram after applying the forward operator \(T_M\). The second term is a prior term, which encourages the reconstruction to look like a plausible biological tomogram.

The key difficulty is that the true prior distribution \(p(x)\) of complete tomograms is unknown, because we do not have access to ground-truth full tomograms. CryoGEN addresses this by introducing an energy-based prior:

\[ p(x)= \frac{1}{Z} \exp(-E_x(x)), \]

where \(E_x(x)\) is an energy function and \(Z\) is the partition function. Low-energy samples correspond to more plausible reconstructions, while high-energy samples correspond to unrealistic or artifact-contaminated reconstructions.

With this prior, the MAP objective can be equivalently viewed as minimizing

\[ \frac{1}{2\sigma_n^2} \left\|T_M(x)-y\right\|_2^2 + E_x(x). \]

This gives a clear interpretation: CryoGEN wants a reconstruction that is both consistent with the measured data and located in a low-energy region of the biological tomogram prior.

A central motivation of CryoGEN is the one-to-many nature of Cryo-ET reconstruction. Because \(T_M\) removes information, two different complete tomograms \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) may produce the same observed tomogram \(y\):

\[ T_M(x_1)=T_M(x_2)=y. \]

If a deterministic model is trained with a simple reconstruction loss, it may learn an averaged solution,

\[ \bar{x}=\frac{1}{2}(x_1+x_2). \]

However, this averaged result may not correspond to a realistic biological structure. In an energy landscape, \(x_1\) and \(x_2\) may both be low-energy plausible solutions, while \(\bar{x}\) lies in a high-energy region between them. This means that the average of plausible solutions is not necessarily plausible.

To address this one-to-many ambiguity, CryoGEN introduces random noise into the reconstructor:

\[ \hat{x}=g_\theta(y+\epsilon_g), \qquad \epsilon_g\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_g^2I). \]

Here, \(g_\theta\) is the prediction model. With the additional noise \(\epsilon_g\), the same observation \(y\) can be mapped to different plausible reconstructions instead of being forced into one deterministic average. In this sense, CryoGEN changes reconstruction from a one-to-one mapping into a conditional generative process.

The remaining question is how to learn the energy prior without ground-truth complete tomograms. CryoGEN makes an important assumption: if a reconstructed \(x\) is plausible, then after rotation and missing-wedge projection, \(T_M(Rx)\) should also look like a real observed tomogram. Therefore, instead of learning the energy directly in the complete tomogram space \(X\), CryoGEN learns it in the observable missing-wedge space \(Y\).

In practice, this energy model is implemented with a GAN-based adversarial formulation. The model compares real observed tomograms \(y+\epsilon\) with generated observations \(T_M(Rg_\theta(y+\epsilon_g))\). Conceptually, the adversarial objective can be written as

\[ \max_\phi \min_\theta \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{|\mathcal{R}|} \sum_{R\in\mathcal{R}} E_\phi \left( T_M(Rg_\theta(y+\epsilon_g)) \right) - E_\phi(y+\epsilon) \right]. \]

Here, \(E_\phi\) acts as the energy model, or discriminator-like prior model. It learns to distinguish real observations from generated ones, while \(g_\theta\) is trained to generate reconstructions whose projected observations match the real tomogram distribution.

CryoGEN also keeps a consistency term similar in spirit to self-supervised reconstruction. This term encourages the predicted tomogram to remain stable after rotation, simulated missing-wedge corruption, and reconstruction:

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{cons}} = \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{|\mathcal{R}|} \sum_{R\in\mathcal{R}} \left\| R^{-1} g_{\theta'} \left( T_M(R(g_\theta(y)+\epsilon_h)) \right) - g_\theta(y) \right\|_2^2 \right]. \]

This term preserves the useful self-supervised signal from IsoNet-like methods, while the energy-based prior provides an additional distribution-level constraint.

Overall, the CryoGEN training objective can be understood as combining three forces:

  • Data consistency, which requires \(T_M(g_\theta(y+\epsilon_g))\) to match the observed \(y\).
  • Self-supervised consistency, which stabilizes reconstruction under rotation and simulated missing-wedge corruption.
  • Energy-based prior matching, which encourages generated reconstructions to lie on the distribution of plausible biological tomograms.

During inference, CryoGEN no longer needs recursive refinement over many iterations. The input tomogram is divided into overlapping subtomograms, each subtomogram is passed through the trained prediction model \(g_\theta\), and the refined subtomograms are reassembled into a complete tomogram. Overlapping regions are averaged to reduce boundary artifacts.

The practical advantage of CryoGEN is that it keeps the efficiency of direct prediction while avoiding the deterministic averaging problem. The random noise improves output diversity, the reconstructor performs self-supervised reconstruction, and the GAN-based energy model evaluates whether the reconstructed prior is realistic enough.

In experiments, CryoGEN produces more complete reconstructions, higher contrast, fewer artifacts, and more consistent Fourier-domain patterns. Compared with recursive methods such as IsoNet, it also significantly reduces training time, achieving around a 10x speedup in reported experiments.

3.4 CryoGEN-II

CryoGEN-II further rethinks Cryo-ET reconstruction from the perspective of variational inference. Its motivation comes from the complementary strengths and weaknesses of IsoNet and CryoGEN. IsoNet can be viewed as a recursive refinement method: it is relatively stable, but the iterative refinement process is slow. CryoGEN formulates reconstruction from a Bayesian maximum a posteriori perspective: it is efficient and generative, but adversarial prior matching can introduce training instability.

The key idea of CryoGEN-II is to put these two views into a unified variational inference framework. In this framework, the observed missing-wedge tomogram \(y\) is treated as the observation, while the complete tomogram \(x\) is treated as a latent variable. Instead of directly predicting a single deterministic reconstruction, the model learns an approximate posterior distribution \(q_\phi(x|y)\), which represents plausible complete tomograms conditioned on the observed incomplete tomogram.

The Cryo-ET forward process is still described by the missing-wedge operator:

\[ y=T_M(x)+\epsilon_n, \qquad \epsilon_n\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_n^2I). \]

Here, \(T_M\) removes the unobserved Fourier-space information caused by limited-angle acquisition. Since \(T_M\) is a many-to-one operator, recovering \(x\) from \(y\) is an ill-posed inverse problem.

From the variational inference perspective, the goal is to maximize the evidence lower bound, or ELBO. Equivalently, this can be written as minimizing a reconstruction consistency term plus a prior matching term:

\[ \frac{1}{2\sigma_n^2} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}(q_\phi) + D_{\mathrm{KL}} \left( q_\phi(x|y)\,\|\,p(x) \right). \]

The first term requires the inferred latent tomogram to reproduce the observed tomogram after applying the missing-wedge operator. This corresponds to the reconstruction consistency idea in IsoNet. The second term requires the inferred posterior to match the prior distribution of plausible complete tomograms, which corresponds to the prior matching idea in CryoGEN.

This gives CryoGEN-II an EM-style interpretation. In the E-step, the model performs prior matching, pushing the inferred latent distribution toward the desired prior distribution. This is conceptually close to CryoGEN. In the M-step, the model performs reconstruction, making sure that the inferred latent tomogram can reproduce the observed missing-wedge tomogram. This is conceptually close to IsoNet.

More concretely, CryoGEN-II adopts a WAE-style formulation. Instead of forcing every sample-level posterior \(q_\phi(x|y)\) to independently match the prior, it matches the aggregated posterior to the prior:

\[ q_\phi(x)= \int q_\phi(x|y)p_{\mathrm{data}}(y)\,dy. \]

A simplified objective can be written as:

\[ \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Y}|} \sum_{y\in\mathcal{Y}} \left\| AE_\phi(y)-y \right\|_2^2 + \lambda D \left( q_\phi(x),p(x) \right). \]

Here, \(E_\phi(y)\) denotes the inferred latent complete tomogram, \(A\) denotes the forward missing-wedge degradation operator, and \(D(q_\phi(x),p(x))\) measures the discrepancy between the aggregated posterior and the prior. The first term is the reconstruction term, while the second term is the prior matching term.

This design is important because matching the aggregated posterior is less restrictive than forcing every individual posterior \(q_\phi(x|y)\) to match the prior exactly. In practice, this helps reduce posterior collapse, improves training stability, and better preserves real structural information.

The divergence term \(D\) can be implemented in different ways. One option is MMD-based matching, which compares distributions through kernel statistics and tends to be stable. Another option is WGAN-based matching, which uses an adversarial critic to distinguish samples from the aggregated posterior and the prior, and often scales better to high-dimensional spaces.

In this formulation, CryoGEN-II combines the advantages of both previous methods. From IsoNet, it inherits the consistency-based reconstruction objective, which stabilizes the learning process. From CryoGEN, it inherits the generative prior matching mechanism, which improves efficiency and avoids purely deterministic reconstruction.

A further improvement in CryoGEN-II is the use of Sharpness-Aware Minimization, or SAM, in the latent prior space. The motivation is that Cryo-ET reconstruction is highly non-convex, and ordinary optimization may converge to sharp minima that are sensitive to small perturbations. A flatter minimum is usually more stable and more likely to preserve robust structural patterns.

For a latent reconstruction \(\tilde{x}_i\), CryoGEN-II first computes the gradient direction:

\[ u_i \leftarrow \nabla_{\tilde{x}_i} L(\tilde{x}_i). \]

Then it perturbs the latent code along the normalized gradient direction:

\[ \tilde{x}_i^+ \leftarrow \tilde{x}_i + \rho \frac{u_i}{\|u_i\|_2}. \]

The model is optimized using the loss evaluated at the perturbed latent code:

\[ L_{\mathrm{SAM}} = L(\tilde{x}_i^+) + \frac{\gamma}{2} \|\phi-\theta\|_2^2. \]

The first term encourages the model to remain accurate even under adversarial latent perturbations, while the second term encourages alignment between the encoder and decoder parameters. In this way, SAM guides the optimization toward flatter regions of the latent loss landscape and improves training robustness.

Overall, CryoGEN-II can be summarized as a variational framework with three key components:

  • A reconstruction term, which ensures that the inferred tomogram remains consistent with the observed missing-wedge tomogram.
  • An aggregated posterior matching term, which aligns the inferred latent distribution with the prior distribution of plausible complete tomograms.
  • A latent-space SAM term, which improves optimization stability and avoids sharp, unstable solutions.

In experiments, CryoGEN-II improves reconstruction stability and structural fidelity compared with CryoGEN. It suppresses artifacts more effectively, preserves sharper structural boundaries, and produces cleaner tomograms on biological datasets such as immature HIV capsids and purified ribosomes.

冷冻电子断层扫描（Cryo-ET）是一种能够在接近原位状态下观察生物结构的重要成像技术。然而，由于倾斜角度受限、电子剂量低以及噪声较强，从二维投影重建高质量三维 tomogram 仍然是一个具有挑战性的逆问题。本文将从 Cryo-ET 的基本工作流程出发，介绍 Weighted Back Projection（WBP）、SIRT 等经典重建方法，并进一步讨论 IsoNet、DeepDeWedge、CryoGEN 和 CryoGEN-II 等 advanced reconstruction methods。

1. Cryo-ET 背景知识

1.1 传统生物学的像素瓶颈

长期以来，光学显微镜是观察生物样本的主要工具。然而，可见光的波长通常在数百纳米量级，因此光学显微镜受到衍射极限的限制。在实际成像中，大约 200 nm 以下的结构很难被清楚分辨。而许多重要的生物对象，例如病毒、蛋白质复合物以及细胞内部的精细结构，都处于纳米尺度，超出了传统光学显微镜的有效观测范围。

1.2 电子显微镜的诞生

为了突破光学显微镜的分辨率限制，研究者开始使用电子束代替可见光进行成像。1931 年，Ernst Ruska 制造了第一台透射电子显微镜（Transmission Electron Microscope, TEM）。由于加速电子的波长远短于可见光，电子显微镜显著提升了显微成像的分辨能力，使人类能够观察到更微观的生物结构。

这对生物学来说是一次重要飞跃。显微观测的尺度从细胞层面进一步推进到纳米尺度，使研究者能够观察病毒、噬菌体以及细胞内部精细结构等传统光学显微镜难以触及的对象。

1.3 TEM 成像：投射而非反射

与许多依赖反射光或发射光的光学成像不同，TEM 的成像方式是投射成像。电子枪发射电子后，电子在高电压下被加速，并穿过一个很薄的样本。当电子穿过样本时，会与样本中的原子发生相互作用。电子密度较高的区域会散射更多电子，因此到达探测器的电子更少，在图像中表现为较暗区域；电子密度较低的区域则允许更多电子穿过，在图像中表现为较亮区域。

然而，单张 TEM 图像本质上仍然是三维物体在电子束方向上的二维投影。样本的深度信息会沿着电子束方向被压缩。因此，虽然 TEM 能够提供高分辨率的二维图像，但单个视角无法直接恢复完整的三维结构。

1.4 从电子显微镜到冷冻电镜

尽管电子显微镜极大提升了分辨率，传统电镜并不天然适合观察生物样本。生物样本非常脆弱：在高能电子束下容易受到辐照损伤；在电镜的真空环境中，水分会蒸发，导致样本塌陷或形态失真。因此，传统电镜很难保持生物结构的天然状态。

冷冻电镜的关键突破是快速冷冻。20 世纪 80 年代，Jacques Dubochet 发展了基于玻璃化的样本制备方法。通过快速降温，样本中的水不会形成晶态冰，而是转变为玻璃态冰。这种方式可以将生物大分子保存在接近天然的含水状态，同时避免冰晶形成对结构造成破坏。

随着直接电子探测器和计算算法的发展，冷冻电镜进一步实现了从大量低剂量、低信噪比图像中重建高分辨率生物结构的能力。

1.5 从二维图像到三维结构

接下来的关键问题是：如何从二维电镜图像恢复三维结构？在冷冻电镜单颗粒分析（Cryo-EM Single Particle Analysis, Cryo-EM SPA）中，研究者会对大量纯化后的同一种分子颗粒进行成像。随后，通过对这些二维颗粒图像进行对齐、分类和平均，可以重建出该分子的高分辨率三维结构。

冷冻电子断层扫描（Cryogenic Electron Tomography, Cryo-ET）采用的是另一种策略。它并不依赖大量纯化后的分子拷贝，而是通过旋转样本，从不同倾斜角度采集一系列投影图像。随后，这些投影图像会被计算重建为三维 tomogram。因此，Cryo-ET 特别适合研究细胞、组织以及原位生物样本。

1.6 为什么 Cryo-ET 重要

Cryo-ET 的主要优势在于，它能够在接近真实的细胞环境中观察生物结构。这对于原位结构生物学非常重要，因为研究目标不仅是解析某个孤立分子的结构，还包括理解它在细胞中的位置、与周围结构的相互作用，以及它在真实细胞环境中的功能。

与 Cryo-EM SPA 相比，Cryo-ET 更适合捕捉稀有事件、低丰度复合物以及异质性的细胞结构。它能够连接分子尺度机制与细胞尺度组织结构，是研究跨尺度生物系统的重要工具。

2. Cryo-ET 工作流程

一个典型的 Cryo-ET 工作流程通常包括五个主要步骤：采样、数据收集、倾斜序列组装、可选的倾斜序列校正，以及 tomogram 重建。

2.1 采样

采样是 Cryo-ET 工作流程的第一步，目标是制备一个足够薄、结构保存良好的生物样本，使其能够在低温条件下被透射电子显微镜成像。由于生物样本通常较厚、含水量高，并且容易受到电子束辐照损伤，因此样本制备对于保留天然结构和后续高质量重建至关重要。

采样过程通常从玻璃化开始。样本会通过高压冷冻或 plunge freezing 被快速冷冻，使水转变为玻璃态冰而不是晶态冰。这样可以避免冰晶对结构造成破坏，并尽可能保留生物大分子的近天然状态。

玻璃化之后，样本会在低温条件下被转移，并进行定位和减薄处理。Cryo-CLEM 可以用于定位感兴趣区域，随后使用 FIB-SEM 去除多余材料。在铣削之前，通常会沉积一层保护层，例如铂或有机金属涂层，以减少样本表面损伤。

最后，通过低电流精细抛光减少 curtaining artifacts，获得表面更加平滑的薄 lamella。最终得到的薄样本片可以用于后续在 TEM 中进行倾斜序列采集。

2.2 数据收集

在薄 lamella 制备完成后，带有冷冻样本的网格会被插入到装有旋转台的透射电子显微镜中。在数据收集过程中，样本会围绕固定的倾斜轴逐步旋转，并在每个倾斜角度记录一张投影图像。Cryo-ET 并不是直接观察完整的三维结构，而是从不同观察方向采集一系列二维投影。

在实际采集中，倾斜角度范围是受限的，通常大约在 −60° 到 +60° 之间。这一限制来自电镜几何结构、样品杆设计，以及高倾斜角下样本等效厚度增加等因素。由于无法采集这个角度范围之外的投影图像，傅里叶空间中的一部分信息会缺失，这也就是后续 tomogram 重建中常说的 missing wedge 问题。

在每个倾斜角度下，电子探测器通常不是只记录一张静态图像，而是记录一个较短的 movie stack。例如，每个投影可能由 3 到 7 帧组成。后续处理时，可以对这些帧进行对齐和叠加，以减少电子束诱导运动的影响，并提高信噪比。

  • Unidirectional 方案。 假设倾斜范围是 −60° 到 +60°，步长为 3°。单向采集方案可以从一端连续采集到另一端，例如 −60°、−57°、−54°、……、0°、……、+57°、+60°。这种方式简单且快速，但接近 0° 的低倾斜图像通常包含较关键信息，却可能在样本已经累积较高电子剂量之后才被采集。
  • Bidirectional 方案。 双向采集方案通常从 0° 附近开始，先采集一侧，再回到另一侧，例如 0°、+3°、+6°、……、+60°，然后采集 −3°、−6°、……、−60°。这种方案可以优先采集低倾斜角图像，从而保护 0° 附近的关键信息。但由于两侧数据是在不同累积剂量条件下采集的，可能会导致倾斜序列两侧的数据质量不对称。
  • Dose-symmetric 方案。 剂量对称采集方案则围绕 0° 在正负角度之间交替采集，例如 0°、+3°、−3°、+6°、−6°、……、+60°、−60°。这种策略可以让正负相对倾斜角下的数据接受更接近的累积剂量，因此数据质量更加对称，通常有利于获得更好的重建结果。不过，它需要旋转台频繁切换方向，对设备稳定性和采集控制提出了更高要求。

总体来说，数据收集决定了最终倾斜序列的角度覆盖范围和噪声水平。这两个因素会直接影响 tomogram 的重建质量：角度覆盖受限会导致 missing wedge 伪影，而低剂量成像会引入较强噪声。因此，数据收集不仅是一个实验步骤，也直接决定了后续计算重建问题的难度。

2.3 倾斜序列组装

在数据收集完成后，每个倾斜角度通常对应的是一个短的 movie stack，而不是单张图像。在这些数据用于后续三维重建之前，需要先校正电子束诱导运动和辐照损伤。在实际工作中，这一步通常由 MotionCor2 和 MotionCor3 等经典运动校正软件完成，它们是目前 cryo-EM 与 Cryo-ET 社区中应用最广泛的方法之一。关于这些软件的更多细节，可以参考 https://emcore.ucsf.edu/ucsf-software。

这些方法通常通过以下几个关键步骤，将原始 movie stack 转换为校正后的投影图像：

  • 全局运动校正（Global Motion Correction）。 在成像过程中，整个视野可能因为电子束诱导运动、机械不稳定、电荷效应或玻璃态冰层形变而发生漂移。首先需要估计每一帧的整体位移，并将所有帧移动到统一坐标系中完成初步对齐。
  • 局部或分块运动校正（Local / Patch-Based Correction）。 实际运动往往不是整张图像完全一致的，不同区域可能以不同方向或不同幅度发生移动。因此，算法会将图像划分为多个 patch，并分别估计每个 patch 的运动轨迹。随后，这些局部运动信息会被整合为覆盖全图的平滑运动场，从而在校正局部形变的同时避免 patch 边界产生人为伪影。
  • 帧叠加（Frame Summation）。 完成运动校正后，同一个 movie stack 中已经对齐的多帧图像会被叠加为一张投影图像。多帧平均能够有效降低随机噪声，同时保留在整个曝光过程中稳定存在的结构信息。
  • 剂量权重处理（Dose Weighting）。 随着电子剂量不断累积，后期帧受到的辐照损伤会逐渐增加，尤其是高频信息衰减更加明显。因此，算法会根据累计剂量为不同帧分配不同的频率权重，在尽可能保留可靠高分辨率信息的同时降低受损信号的影响。

经过上述处理后，每个倾斜角度最终对应一张校正后的投影图像。这些投影图像会按照采集角度进行排序和组装，形成最终的 tilt series，并作为后续 tilt-series alignment 和 tomographic reconstruction 的输入。

2.4 倾斜序列校正

倾斜序列校正是 Cryo-ET 流程中一个可选但重要的预处理步骤。它的主要目标是在 tomographic reconstruction 之前，校正电镜成像系统对图像信号引入的调制和失真。其中最重要的校正之一是 CTF 校正，CTF 指的是对比度转换函数（contrast transfer function）。

CTF 的来源

在 cryo-EM 和 Cryo-ET 成像中，电镜通常会被有意设置为轻微离焦状态。离焦可以增强相位对比度，使弱散射的生物样本更容易被观察到。但同时，离焦也会使散射波和未散射波在到达探测器之前积累不同的相位差。

电镜光学系统对不同空间频率信号的调制作用可以用对比度转换函数来描述。CTF 的一种常见形式为

\[ \mathrm{CTF}(k)= \left[ -\sqrt{1-A^2}\sin \chi(k) +A\cos \chi(k) \right] \cdot e^{-Bk^2/4}. \]

其中，\(k\) 表示空间频率，\(A\) 是振幅对比度比例，\(e^{-Bk^2/4}\) 是包络项，用于描述高频信息的衰减。相位函数 \(\chi(k)\) 通常由离焦量、电子波长和球差等参数决定：

\[ \chi(k)= \pi \lambda \Delta f k^2 +\frac{\pi}{2}C_s\lambda^3 k^4. \]

其中，\(\lambda\) 表示电子波长，\(\Delta f\) 表示离焦量，\(C_s\) 表示球差系数。

CTF 主要带来两个问题。第一，在某些空间频率处，CTF 的值可能为负，这会导致相位反转，即原本的亮暗密度对比被翻转。第二，包络项会抑制高频信号，使精细结构信息变弱或变模糊。

用于 CTF 校正的 Wiener Filter

为了理解 CTF 校正，可以将成像过程写成傅里叶空间中的形式：

\[ Y(k)=H(k)X(k)+N(k), \]

其中，\(Y(k)\) 表示观测图像的傅里叶表示，\(X(k)\) 表示未知的真实信号，\(H(k)\) 表示 CTF，\(N(k)\) 表示噪声。

最直接的逆滤波方法可以写作

\[ \hat{X}(k)=\frac{Y(k)}{H(k)}. \]

然而，这种直接相除是不稳定的。当 \(H(k)\) 接近 0 时，即使很小的噪声也会被严重放大。因此，实际中通常不会直接除以 CTF，而是使用 Wiener filter，在恢复信号和抑制噪声之间进行平衡。

假设我们用一个线性滤波器 \(G(k)\) 来估计 \(X(k)\)：

\[ \hat{X}(k)=G(k)Y(k). \]

我们的目标是选择合适的 \(G(k)\)，使均方误差最小：

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[|\hat{X}(k)-X(k)|^2\right]. \]

将 \(Y(k)=H(k)X(k)+N(k)\) 代入，可以得到

\[ \hat{X}(k)-X(k)= (G(k)H(k)-1)X(k)+G(k)N(k). \]

假设真实信号和噪声不相关，则期望误差可以写成

\[ \mathbb{E}\left[|\hat{X}(k)-X(k)|^2\right] = |G(k)H(k)-1|^2 S_X(k) + |G(k)|^2 S_N(k), \]

其中，\(S_X(k)\) 和 \(S_N(k)\) 分别表示真实信号和噪声的功率谱。

对 \(G(k)\) 最小化上述误差，可以得到 Wiener filter：

\[ G(k)= \frac{H^*(k)S_X(k)} {|H(k)|^2S_X(k)+S_N(k)}. \]

等价地，可以写成

\[ G(k)= \frac{H^*(k)} {|H(k)|^2+\frac{S_N(k)}{S_X(k)}}. \]

因此，经过 Wiener filter 校正后的估计为

\[ \hat{X}(k)= \frac{H^*(k)} {|H(k)|^2+\frac{S_N(k)}{S_X(k)}} Y(k). \]

当 \(H(k)\) 较大且信噪比较高时，Wiener filter 的行为接近直接逆滤波；当 \(H(k)\) 较小或噪声较强时，分母中的正则项会避免噪声被过度放大。因此，Wiener filter 比直接 CTF 反卷积更加稳定。

2.5 Tomogram 重建

在完成采样、数据收集、倾斜序列组装以及可选校正之后，Cryo-ET 的最终目标是从一系列二维投影图像中重建出三维 tomogram。这是整个工作流程中最核心的计算步骤：给定不同倾斜角度下的投影图像，我们希望恢复样本真实的三维结构。

加权反投影

断层重建的数学基础是中心切片定理。以二维情况为例，假设待重建物体为 \(f(x,y)\)，在角度 \(\theta\) 下的投影为 \(p_\theta(t)\)。这个投影可以理解为沿着与投影方向垂直的直线对物体进行积分：

\[ p_\theta(t)= \int\int f(x,y)\, \delta(t-x\cos\theta-y\sin\theta)\,dx\,dy. \]

对该投影做一维傅里叶变换，可以得到

\[ P_\theta(k)= \int p_\theta(t)e^{-i2\pi kt}\,dt. \]

根据中心切片定理，这个结果恰好等于原物体二维傅里叶变换中经过原点的一条切片：

\[ P_\theta(k)= F(k\cos\theta,k\sin\theta). \]

换句话说，每一张投影图像都提供了傅里叶空间中的一条径向切片。如果能够从足够多的角度采集投影，就可以将这些傅里叶切片组合起来恢复原始物体。

最直接的想法是反投影。对于每个投影 \(p_\theta(t)\)，将它沿对应的投影方向“铺回”到空间中，并把所有角度的贡献相加：

\[ f_{\mathrm{BP}}(x,y)= \int_0^\pi p_\theta(x\cos\theta+y\sin\theta) \,d\theta. \]

然而，简单反投影会得到模糊的重建结果。原因在于傅里叶空间中的采样并不均匀。所有径向切片都会经过原点，因此原点附近的低频信息被重复采样多次，而远离原点的高频信息采样更加稀疏。这会导致低频成分被过度强调，使重建图像变得模糊。

加权反投影（Weighted Back Projection, WBP）通过在反投影之前加入频域滤波来校正这种采样不均衡。最常见的滤波器是 ramp filter，它会将每个投影的频谱乘上 \(|k|\)。这样可以抑制被过度采样的低频成分，并增强高频细节：

\[ \tilde{P}_\theta(k)= |k|P_\theta(k). \]

滤波之后，重建图像可以写作

\[ f(x,y)= \int_0^\pi \left[ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P_\theta(k)|k| e^{i2\pi k(x\cos\theta+y\sin\theta)} \,dk \right] d\theta. \]

这就是 WBP 的基本思想：先在傅里叶空间中对每张投影进行滤波，再将滤波后的投影按照对应角度反投影回实空间，并对所有角度进行累加。

同步迭代重建方法

另一类经典重建方法是同步迭代重建方法（Simultaneous Iterative Reconstruction Technique, SIRT）。与基于解析反演的 WBP 不同，SIRT 将 tomogram 重建写成一个线性逆问题。

离散化之后，未知的 tomogram 可以表示为向量 \(x\)，实际观测到的投影数据可以表示为向量 \(b\)。成像过程可以建模为

\[ Ax=b, \]

其中，\(A\) 是系统投影矩阵。矩阵 \(A\) 的每一行描述一条投影射线如何穿过体数据中的 voxels，而元素 \(a_{ij}\) 表示第 \(j\) 个 voxel 对第 \(i\) 条射线测量值的贡献。

在实际中，\(A\) 通常非常大、稀疏且病态，并且方程数量与未知数数量往往并不完全相等。因此，直接求解 \(A^{-1}b\) 是不可行的。通常会将重建问题转化为最小二乘问题：

\[ \min_x f(x)= \frac{1}{2}\|Ax-b\|_2^2. \]

该目标函数的梯度为

\[ \nabla f(x)= A^T(Ax-b). \]

因此，最基本的梯度下降更新可以写成

\[ x^{(k+1)} = x^{(k)} + \lambda A^T(b-Ax^{(k)}), \]

其中，\(b-Ax^{(k)}\) 表示当前重建结果与真实投影之间的残差，\(\lambda\) 是步长。

SIRT 进一步引入行归一化和列归一化，用来平衡不同射线和不同 voxel 的贡献。令 \(R\) 表示由 \(A\) 的行和组成的对角矩阵，\(C\) 表示由 \(A\) 的列和组成的对角矩阵：

\[ R_{ii}=\sum_j a_{ij}, \qquad C_{jj}=\sum_i a_{ij}. \]

加入这些归一化项之后，SIRT 的迭代更新可以写作

\[ x^{(k+1)} = x^{(k)} + \lambda C^{-1}A^TR^{-1} \left(b-Ax^{(k)}\right). \]

这个更新式有一个直观解释。首先，将当前估计 \(x^{(k)}\) 进行前向投影，得到 \(Ax^{(k)}\)。然后，计算真实投影 \(b\) 与预测投影 \(Ax^{(k)}\) 之间的差值。最后，将这个残差反投影回体数据中，用来更新 tomogram。不断重复这一过程，就可以逐步减小投影误差。

与 WBP 相比，SIRT 通常能够得到更平滑的重建结果，并在一定程度上减少条纹状伪影。但它的计算代价更高，因为需要反复进行前向投影和反投影迭代。

为什么经典重建方法不够好

尽管 WBP 和 SIRT 是断层重建中的基础方法，但它们并不能完全解决 Cryo-ET 中的重建困难。第一个主要问题来自数据收集。由于样本通常只能在有限角度范围内倾斜，例如大约 −60° 到 +60°，许多方向上的投影数据无法被采集。

在傅里叶空间中，这种有限角度采集会形成 missing wedge，也就是一块楔形的未采样频率区域。由于这些信息从未被实际测量，经典重建算法无法仅凭数据本身真正恢复它们。因此，重建出的 tomogram 往往存在各向异性分辨率、拉伸伪影以及结构细节缺失等问题。

第二个问题是噪声。Cryo-ET 必须使用较低电子剂量，以避免破坏生物样本结构，但低剂量成像会导致信噪比较低。在重建过程中，噪声可能被放大或传播到整个体数据中，使最终 tomogram 呈现较强噪声和较低对比度。

WBP 快速且简单，但对角度缺失和噪声较为敏感。SIRT 更加稳健，通常能得到更平滑的结果，但它仍然无法恢复倾斜序列中本来就没有被采集到的信息。因此，这两类方法都受到 Cryo-ET 数据采集物理限制的约束。

这也是为什么需要更先进的重建方法。为了更好地恢复结构细节、减少伪影，并支持下游生物分析任务，近年来的重建方法开始将深度学习、自监督学习、去噪策略和生成式先验引入 Cryo-ET reconstruction。

3. 先进重建方法

3.1 IsoNet

IsoNet 是一种基于自监督学习的深度学习方法，主要用于减轻 missing wedge 伪影，并提升 Cryo-ET tomogram 的各向同性分辨率。它不需要干净的 ground truth，也不依赖 subtomogram averaging，而是直接从原始 tomogram 中学习结构信息。

IsoNet 的流程主要包括五步：可选的 CTF 反卷积、可选的 mask 生成、subtomogram 提取、迭代训练，以及预测。CTF 反卷积可以增强图像对比度，mask 生成则用于排除冰、碳膜或真空等空白区域。随后，算法会从包含有效结构信息的区域中提取 subtomograms，用于后续自监督训练。

IsoNet 的核心思想是从 tomogram 自身构造训练样本对。对于一个提取出的 subtomogram \(x\)，IsoNet 首先将其旋转到新的方向：

\[ x_r = R_r x, \]

其中，\(R_r\) 表示三维旋转算子。

随后，算法会在傅里叶空间中施加一个额外的人造 missing wedge mask \(M_a\)，生成退化后的输入：

\[ \tilde{x}_r = \mathcal{F}^{-1} \left[ M_a \cdot \mathcal{F}(x_r) \right], \]

其中，\(\mathcal{F}\) 和 \(\mathcal{F}^{-1}\) 分别表示傅里叶变换和傅里叶逆变换。退化后的 subtomogram \(\tilde{x}_r\) 作为输入，而旋转后的 subtomogram \(x_r\) 作为目标。

神经网络 \(f_\theta\) 通过最小化重建损失来学习恢复被人为去除的信息：

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{IsoNet}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x,r} \left[ \left\| f_\theta(\tilde{x}_r)-x_r \right\|_2^2 \right]. \]

通过这种方式，IsoNet 学习的是从“信息更缺失”的 subtomogram 到“信息相对更完整”的 subtomogram 的映射，而不需要外部干净标签。

这个训练过程是迭代进行的。每一轮迭代后，训练好的网络会预测经过校正的 subtomograms。需要注意的是，IsoNet 不会覆盖原本已经观测到的傅里叶信息，而是只将新预测出的信息补充到原始 missing wedge 区域中。如果 \(M_o\) 表示原始已观测到的傅里叶区域，那么更新过程可以概念性地写作：

\[ \mathcal{F}(x^{(t+1)}) = M_o \cdot \mathcal{F}(x^{(0)}) + (1-M_o)\cdot \mathcal{F}(\hat{x}^{(t)}), \]

其中，\(x^{(0)}\) 是原始 subtomogram，\(\hat{x}^{(t)}\) 是第 \(t\) 轮迭代中网络预测得到的结果。

通过这种递归式 refinement，missing wedge 伪影会逐渐减弱，重建结果也会变得更加各向同性。在实际效果上，IsoNet 可以减轻伪影、提高对比度，并使关键结构更容易被观察和解释。例如，在原始 tomogram 中被拉伸、模糊或不完整的结构，经过 IsoNet 处理后可能变得更加完整和清晰。

3.2 DeepDeWedge

DeepDeWedge 是另一种用于 Cryo-ET 重建的自监督深度学习方法。与 IsoNet 相比，它的重点不仅是补偿 missing wedge，还希望同时完成去噪。换句话说，DeepDeWedge 的目标是在恢复缺失结构信息的同时，得到更干净、对比度更高的 tomogram。

它的核心思想来自 Noise2Noise：如果两份观测包含相同的真实信号，但噪声相互独立，那么神经网络可以在没有干净 ground truth 的情况下，学习从一个带噪观测映射到另一个带噪观测。在 Cryo-ET 中，DeepDeWedge 通过将原始 tilt series 拆分成两个子倾斜序列来构造这种配对数据，例如 even/odd split。

整体流程可以概括为：

  • 原始 tilt series 首先被拆分为两个子倾斜序列，记为 \(t^0\) 和 \(t^1\)。随后分别对这两个子序列进行 filtered back projection，得到两个带噪 tomogram。它们包含相同的生物结构，但噪声模式不同。
  • 从这两个重建结果中提取配对的 subtomograms \((v_i^0, v_i^1)\)。这些配对数据提供了自监督训练信号：其中一个 half-reconstruction 可以作为输入，另一个 half-reconstruction 作为目标。
  • 在模型训练过程中，DeepDeWedge 会对配对 subtomograms 施加旋转和傅里叶空间 mask。这样，网络可以同时学习如何抑制噪声，以及如何补全 missing wedge 区域。一个简化的训练目标可以写作：
\[ \min_\theta \sum_i \left\| f_\theta(\tilde{v}_i^0)-\tilde{v}_i^1 \right\|_2^2, \]

其中，\(\tilde{v}_i^0\) 和 \(\tilde{v}_i^1\) 表示经过旋转和 mask 处理后的输入-目标样本对，\(f_\theta\) 表示神经网络。

  • 训练收敛后，训练好的模型会被用于 refine subtomograms，并将这些 subtomograms 重新组装成 tomograms。最后，将两个 refined half-reconstructions 进行平均，得到最终重建结果。

DeepDeWedge 的优势在于，它将两个原本相对分离的目标结合在一起：去噪和 missing wedge 重建。相比于先使用 denoiser 再使用 missing wedge correction 方法的两步流程，DeepDeWedge 提供了一个统一的自监督框架，通常能够得到更干净、整体对比度更强的 tomogram。

3.3 CryoGEN

CryoGEN 从概率建模和生成式建模的角度重新思考 Cryo-ET 重建。它并不把 missing wedge correction 仅仅看作一个确定性的 image-to-image restoration 问题，而是将 Cryo-ET 重建建模为一个具有多个潜在解的病态逆问题。

令 \(y\) 表示观测到的 missing-wedge tomogram，\(x\) 表示希望恢复的完整 tomogram。前向退化过程可以写作：

\[ y=T_M(x)+\epsilon_n, \qquad \epsilon_n\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_n^2I), \]

其中，\(T_M\) 是 missing-wedge operator，\(\epsilon_n\) 表示成像噪声。直观上，\(T_M\) 会根据有限倾斜角度移除傅里叶空间中的一部分信息。

根据贝叶斯公式，完整 tomogram 的后验分布可以写成：

\[ \log p(x|y)=\log p(y|x)+\log p(x)+C. \]

由于噪声被假设为高斯噪声，似然项可以写成一个 data-consistency term：

\[ \log p(y|x)= -\frac{1}{2\sigma_n^2} \left\|T_M(x)-y\right\|_2^2 +C. \]

因此，最大后验估计（maximum a posteriori, MAP）形式的重建可以写作：

\[ x^*(y)= \arg\max_x \left[ -\frac{1}{2\sigma_n^2} \left\|T_M(x)-y\right\|_2^2 +\log p(x) \right]. \]

这个公式包含两部分。第一项要求重建出的 tomogram 在经过前向算子 \(T_M\) 之后，能够与观测到的 missing-wedge tomogram 保持一致。第二项是先验项，它要求重建结果看起来像一个合理的生物 tomogram。

关键困难在于，完整 tomogram 的真实先验分布 \(p(x)\) 是未知的，因为我们无法获得 ground-truth full tomograms。CryoGEN 使用 energy-based prior 来刻画这一先验分布：

\[ p(x)= \frac{1}{Z} \exp(-E_x(x)), \]

其中，\(E_x(x)\) 是能量函数，\(Z\) 是配分函数。低能量样本对应更合理的重建结果，而高能量样本则对应不真实或包含伪影的重建结果。

引入这个先验之后，MAP 目标也可以等价地看成最小化：

\[ \frac{1}{2\sigma_n^2} \left\|T_M(x)-y\right\|_2^2 + E_x(x). \]

这个式子给出了非常直观的解释：CryoGEN 希望得到的重建结果既要和观测数据一致，又要位于生物 tomogram 先验分布中的低能量区域。

CryoGEN 的核心动机之一，是 Cryo-ET 重建本质上的一对多问题。由于 \(T_M\) 会移除信息，两个不同的完整 tomogram \(x_1\) 和 \(x_2\) 可能对应同一个观测 tomogram \(y\)：

\[ T_M(x_1)=T_M(x_2)=y. \]

如果使用简单重建损失训练一个确定性模型，模型可能会学到一个平均化的解：

\[ \bar{x}=\frac{1}{2}(x_1+x_2). \]

然而，这个平均结果并不一定对应一个真实合理的生物结构。在能量景观中，\(x_1\) 和 \(x_2\) 可能都是低能量的合理解，但 \(\bar{x}\) 可能位于它们之间的高能量区域。也就是说，多个合理解的平均并不一定仍然合理。

为了解决这种一对多歧义，CryoGEN 在 reconstructor 中引入随机噪声：

\[ \hat{x}=g_\theta(y+\epsilon_g), \qquad \epsilon_g\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_g^2I). \]

其中，\(g_\theta\) 是预测模型。加入噪声 \(\epsilon_g\) 后，同一个观测 \(y\) 可以被映射到不同的合理重建结果，而不是被迫输出一个确定性的平均解。从这个意义上说，CryoGEN 将重建过程从一对一映射转化为条件生成过程。

剩下的问题是：在没有 ground-truth complete tomograms 的情况下，如何学习这个能量先验？CryoGEN 使用了一个重要假设：如果某个重建出的 \(x\) 是合理的，那么它经过旋转和 missing-wedge 投影之后得到的 \(T_M(Rx)\)，也应该看起来像一个真实观测到的 tomogram。因此，CryoGEN 并不直接在完整 tomogram 空间 \(X\) 中学习能量，而是在可观测的 missing-wedge 空间 \(Y\) 中学习。

在实际实现中，这个能量模型通过 GAN-based adversarial formulation 来实现。模型会比较真实观测 tomogram \(y+\epsilon\) 和由 reconstructor 生成后再投影得到的观测 \(T_M(Rg_\theta(y+\epsilon_g))\)。概念上，对抗目标可以写作：

\[ \max_\phi \min_\theta \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{|\mathcal{R}|} \sum_{R\in\mathcal{R}} E_\phi \left( T_M(Rg_\theta(y+\epsilon_g)) \right) - E_\phi(y+\epsilon) \right]. \]

其中，\(E_\phi\) 扮演 energy model，也可以理解为类似 discriminator 的先验模型。它学习区分真实观测和模型生成的观测，而 \(g_\theta\) 则被训练成生成这样的重建结果：经过 \(T_M\) 和旋转投影之后，其分布能够匹配真实观测 tomogram 的分布。

CryoGEN 也保留了一个与自监督重建思想类似的 consistency term。这个项鼓励预测出的 tomogram 在经过旋转、人造 missing-wedge 退化和再次重建后仍然保持一致：

\[ \mathcal{L}_{\text{cons}} = \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{|\mathcal{R}|} \sum_{R\in\mathcal{R}} \left\| R^{-1} g_{\theta'} \left( T_M(R(g_\theta(y)+\epsilon_h)) \right) - g_\theta(y) \right\|_2^2 \right]. \]

这一项保留了 IsoNet 类方法中有用的自监督信号，而 energy-based prior 则进一步提供了分布层面的约束。

总体来说，CryoGEN 的训练目标可以理解为三种力量的结合：

  • 数据一致性，即要求 \(T_M(g_\theta(y+\epsilon_g))\) 与观测到的 \(y\) 保持一致。
  • 自监督一致性，即在旋转和模拟 missing-wedge 退化下保持重建稳定。
  • 基于能量的先验匹配，即鼓励生成的重建结果落在合理生物 tomogram 的分布上。

在推理阶段，CryoGEN 不再需要进行大量递归式 refinement。输入 tomogram 会被划分为多个重叠的 subtomograms，每个 subtomogram 经过训练好的预测模型 \(g_\theta\) 得到校正结果，随后这些 subtomograms 会被重新组装成完整 tomogram。重叠区域会进行加权平均，以减少边界伪影。

CryoGEN 的实际优势在于，它保留了直接预测的效率，同时避免了确定性平均带来的问题。随机噪声提高了输出多样性，reconstructor 负责自监督重建，而 GAN-based energy model 则负责判断重建结果对应的先验是否足够真实。

在实验中，CryoGEN 能够得到更完整的重建结果、更高的对比度、更少的伪影，以及在傅里叶域中更加一致的频率分布。与 IsoNet 这类递归式方法相比，它还显著减少了训练时间，在论文实验中实现了约 10 倍的加速。

3.4 CryoGEN-II

CryoGEN-II 从变分推断的角度进一步重新思考 Cryo-ET 重建。它的动机来自 IsoNet 和 CryoGEN 的互补性。IsoNet 可以看作一种 recursive refinement 方法：训练相对稳定，但迭代 refinement 过程较慢。CryoGEN 则从贝叶斯最大后验的角度建模重建问题：它具有较高效率和生成能力，但对抗式 prior matching 可能带来训练不稳定。

在介绍 formulation 之前，可以先用下面的表格总结二者的互补特点：

属性 CryoGEN IsoNet CryoGEN-II
训练稳定性 × ✓ ✓
快速收敛 ✓ × ✓
显式潜在变量模型 ✓ × ✓
基于一致性的重建目标 × ✓ ✓

CryoGEN-II 的核心思想是将这两种视角放入统一的变分推断框架中。在这个框架中，观测到的 missing-wedge tomogram \(y\) 被看作观测变量，而完整 tomogram \(x\) 被看作潜在变量。模型不再只是直接预测一个确定性的重建结果，而是学习一个近似后验分布 \(q_\phi(x|y)\)，表示在给定不完整观测 \(y\) 的条件下可能存在的完整 tomograms。

Cryo-ET 的前向过程仍然由 missing-wedge operator 描述：

\[ y=T_M(x)+\epsilon_n, \qquad \epsilon_n\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_n^2I). \]

其中，\(T_M\) 会移除由于有限角度采集导致的未观测傅里叶信息。由于 \(T_M\) 是一个 many-to-one operator，从 \(y\) 恢复 \(x\) 是一个病态逆问题。

从变分推断角度看，目标是最大化 evidence lower bound，也就是 ELBO。等价地，这可以写成最小化一个 reconstruction consistency term 加上一个 prior matching term：

\[ \frac{1}{2\sigma_n^2} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cons}}(q_\phi) + D_{\mathrm{KL}} \left( q_\phi(x|y)\,\|\,p(x) \right). \]

第一项要求推断出的潜在 tomogram 在经过 missing-wedge operator 后能够重新生成观测 tomogram，这对应 IsoNet 中的 reconstruction consistency 思想。第二项要求推断出的后验分布匹配合理完整 tomograms 的先验分布，这对应 CryoGEN 中的 prior matching 思想。

因此，CryoGEN-II 可以被理解为一种 EM-style 的迭代框架。在 E-step 中，模型进行 prior matching，将推断出的潜在分布推向目标先验分布，这在思想上接近 CryoGEN。在 M-step 中，模型进行 reconstruction，确保推断出的潜在 tomogram 能够重新生成观测到的 missing-wedge tomogram，这在思想上接近 IsoNet。

更具体地说，CryoGEN-II 使用 WAE-style formulation。它并不强制每个样本级别的后验 \(q_\phi(x|y)\) 都单独匹配先验，而是让聚合后验匹配先验：

\[ q_\phi(x)= \int q_\phi(x|y)p_{\mathrm{data}}(y)\,dy. \]

一个简化的目标函数可以写成：

\[ \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Y}|} \sum_{y\in\mathcal{Y}} \left\| AE_\phi(y)-y \right\|_2^2 + \lambda D \left( q_\phi(x),p(x) \right). \]

其中，\(E_\phi(y)\) 表示推断出的潜在完整 tomogram，\(A\) 表示前向 missing-wedge 退化算子，\(D(q_\phi(x),p(x))\) 表示聚合后验与先验分布之间的差异。第一项是重建项，第二项是先验匹配项。

这种设计很重要，因为匹配聚合后验比分别强制每个 \(q_\phi(x|y)\) 精确匹配先验更加灵活。在实际训练中，这有助于减少 posterior collapse，提高训练稳定性，并更好地保留真实结构信息。

散度项 \(D\) 可以用不同方式实现。一种选择是 MMD-based matching，它通过 kernel statistics 比较两个分布，通常训练较稳定。另一种选择是 WGAN-based matching，它使用 adversarial critic 区分来自聚合后验和先验的样本，通常更适合高维空间。

在这个 formulation 中，CryoGEN-II 结合了前两类方法的优势。它从 IsoNet 中继承了 consistency-based reconstruction objective，从而提升训练稳定性；又从 CryoGEN 中继承了 generative prior matching mechanism，从而提高效率，并避免纯确定性重建的局限。

CryoGEN-II 的另一个重要改进，是在潜在先验空间中引入 Sharpness-Aware Minimization，也就是 SAM。其动机在于，Cryo-ET 重建问题高度非凸，普通优化过程可能收敛到对小扰动非常敏感的尖锐极小值。相比之下，更平坦的极小值通常更加稳定，也更有利于保留稳健的结构模式。

对于一个潜在重建 \(\tilde{x}_i\)，CryoGEN-II 首先计算梯度方向：

\[ u_i \leftarrow \nabla_{\tilde{x}_i} L(\tilde{x}_i). \]

随后，它沿归一化梯度方向对潜在变量进行扰动：

\[ \tilde{x}_i^+ \leftarrow \tilde{x}_i + \rho \frac{u_i}{\|u_i\|_2}. \]

模型随后使用扰动后的潜在变量来计算并优化损失：

\[ L_{\mathrm{SAM}} = L(\tilde{x}_i^+) + \frac{\gamma}{2} \|\phi-\theta\|_2^2. \]

第一项要求模型在潜在变量受到对抗扰动后仍然保持准确，第二项则用于鼓励 encoder 和 decoder 参数之间的一致性。通过这种方式，SAM 会引导优化过程走向潜在损失景观中更平坦的区域，从而提高训练鲁棒性。

总体来看，CryoGEN-II 可以概括为一个包含三个关键部分的变分框架：

  • 重建项：确保推断出的 tomogram 与观测到的 missing-wedge tomogram 保持一致。
  • 聚合后验匹配项：使推断出的潜在分布与合理完整 tomogram 的先验分布对齐。
  • 潜在空间 SAM 项：提高优化稳定性，避免收敛到尖锐且不稳定的解。

在实验中，相比 CryoGEN，CryoGEN-II 进一步提高了重建稳定性和结构保真度。它能够更有效地抑制伪影，保留更清晰的结构边界，并在 immature HIV capsids 和 purified ribosomes 等生物数据集上得到更加干净的 tomogram。
